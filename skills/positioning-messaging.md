# Positioning & Messaging

The messaging skill doesn't pick a single methodology. Different messaging jobs call for different frameworks, and using the wrong one is how messaging work goes sideways. The skill diagnoses the job first, then routes to the right framework.

## The problem it solves

Most AI-assisted messaging work fails because it treats "help with positioning" as one job. It's not. Building positioning from scratch, rewriting a homepage, crafting a launch narrative, sharpening a sales pitch, and writing a tagline are five different jobs requiring five different frameworks. Use the wrong one and the output is structurally wrong no matter how well-written it is.

## The routing logic

The skill identifies which job the user is actually asking for:

| Job | Framework | Source |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| Build or rebuild strategic positioning | Dunford — *Obviously Awesome* | April Dunford |
| Rewrite a homepage or one-pager | Fletch PMM | Pierri & Kaminski |
| Build a launch narrative or category story | Strategic narrative arc | Andy Raskin |
| Sharpen a sales pitch or deck | Sales pitch arc | Dunford — *Sales Pitch* |
| Craft a tagline or differentiator one-liner | Onlyness statement | Marty Neumeier |
| Translate positioning into persona-specific language | Message house + persona matrix | Hybrid |

If the request spans multiple jobs, the skill runs workflows in a specific order: positioning → narrative → homepage → pitch → tagline. Strategy before expression, narrative before deck, deck before one-liner.

## The six workflows

**Dunford positioning (Obviously Awesome).** The foundation. Works through ten steps in order: understand best-fit customers, list true competitive alternatives (including do-nothing, agencies, and in-house), isolate unique attributes, map attributes to value themes, determine who cares most, find the right market frame, layer on a trend, capture internally, translate externally, and pressure-test.

The step most teams skip is competitive alternatives. Dunford's insight is that the buyer's real consideration set is not just named competitors. It includes the status quo, in-house teams, agencies, and adjacent tools. Positioning built only against named competitors misses the actual deal dynamics.

**Fletch homepage messaging (Pierri & Kaminski).** Boils down to one discipline: one product, one audience, one problem. Most homepages fail because they try to be all things to all people. The skill enforces the PMF lens (what does it do, who is it for, why is it better), builds the hero stack, and catches common failures: feature soup, audience confusion, vague headlines, generic visuals, CTA pile-ups.

**Raskin strategic narrative.** Five parts in order: name the undeniable shift, show the stakes, tease the promised land, position the product as "magic gifts," and show evidence. The product enters at step four, not step one. This is the framework for launches, keynotes, and category-defining moments.

**Dunford sales pitch arc.** A refinement of Raskin tuned for one-to-one sales conversations. Leads with a contrarian insight about the buyer's world, describes the old way and why it fails, introduces the new way (not yet the product), then positions the product as the best at the new way, with proof.

**Neumeier onlyness statement.** A forcing function: "Our [offering] is the only [category] that [unique value]." If you can't fill in the blanks, the positioning isn't tight enough. Includes the substitution test: replace your company name with a competitor's. If it still reads true, it's not onlyness.

**Persona messaging matrix.** Translates locked positioning into channel- and persona-specific language. Each persona gets a headline, top pain points, the pillars that map to those pains, a value statement, and objection responses.

## The stress tests

Every output runs through two stress tests before it ships:

**Sharp stress test (mental availability).** Does the messaging name specific buying moments (Category Entry Points), or stay abstract? Does it use distinctive brand assets buyers already associate with us, or restart recognition from zero? Will it land for a light buyer who hasn't heard from us in six months, or does it assume context they don't have?

**Laja stress test (5-second clarity).** Can someone in the ICP read the headline and subhead in 5 seconds and say what it does, who it's for, and why it matters? For every claim, does the "so what" chain reach a concrete business outcome within three steps? Does the specificity hold up when you replace every adjective with a metric or proof point? Does the competitor substitution test pass?

If the Sharp test fails (abstract triggers, no distinctive assets, only legible to engaged buyers), the messaging may be intellectually correct but won't grow the brand. If the Laja test fails (unclear, unspecific, undifferentiated), it won't register at all.

## What this looks like in practice

For an AI feature launch into a skeptical creative audience, the skill routed to the Raskin narrative arc for the launch story, then Dunford positioning for the strategic foundation, then the persona messaging matrix to split the narrative between two audiences with opposing relationships to AI.

The Sharp stress test caught that the initial messaging assumed buyers were already bought into AI as a creative tool (they weren't). The Laja test caught that the headline was a claim any competitor could make verbatim. Both rewrites happened before the messaging left the building.

Result: persona-specific positioning that turned audience resistance into adoption, with the narrative now anchoring how every function talks to customers about the product.
