# Pricing & Packaging Strategy

A pricing skill grounded in Kyle Poyar's PLG pricing operator playbook, covering the PMM's role in pricing: the strategic, market-facing side, not the financial modeling finance owns.

## The problem it solves

Pricing decisions in B2B SaaS are often made by feel or by copying competitors. The result is packaging that doesn't scale with customer value, tiers that don't map to real use cases, and PLG free trials with no clear conversion mechanic. PMMs own the market-facing pricing story but rarely have a structured framework for making the underlying decisions.

## The workflow

The skill routes to the right workflow based on the pricing problem:

| Problem | Workflow |
|---------|---------|
| New product or feature pricing | Value metric selection → packaging design |
| Tier restructuring | Tier structure design |
| Price increase | Price increase communication playbook |
| Competitive response | Competitive pricing response |
| Pricing page design | Pricing page guidance |
| PLG free trial or reverse trial | PLG pricing mechanics |

## Value metric framework (Poyar)

The most important pricing decision is the value metric: the unit that scales with customer value. The skill scores candidate metrics against Poyar's criteria:

- **Easy for buyer to estimate:** Can they predict what they'll pay? (Too unpredictable = pricing friction)
- **Scales with value:** Does more of this metric mean the customer is getting more value?
- **Aligned with willingness to pay:** Does it grow with the buyer's budget or P&L line item?
- **Hard for competitors to undercut:** Can a rival offer the same metric at a 50% discount without destroying their unit economics?
- **Defensible at the buyer level:** Will procurement accept it?

Candidate metric types include per-seat, usage-based, outcome-based, hybrid, and flat. Each has a specific "best when" profile. The skill scores all candidates, picks the winner, and pressure-tests against the ICP's mental model (enterprise procurement thinks in annual contracts, PLG users think in try-then-upgrade).

## Packaging principles (Poyar)

- Packages built around use cases, not feature lists
- Each tier has a clear "this is for you if..." profile
- Features escalate in ways that match how customers grow
- The middle tier should be the obvious right choice for the modal buyer (avoid the Goldilocks anti-pattern where all three tiers feel equally compelling)
- Every tier has a natural expansion trigger to the next

## PLG pricing mechanics (Poyar)

For companies running PLG alongside sales-led motions, the skill designs the trial-to-paid conversion path:

**Model selection:** Free trial (time-bound), freemium (forever-free), or reverse trial (full features for X days, then downgrade to limited free). Each has a specific "best for" profile and risk.

**Activation metric:** The most important PLG number, per Poyar, is what % of signups reach the "aha moment" within X days. The skill defines the metric and benchmarks against industry data by ACV band.

**Conversion mechanic:** Time-based (trial expires), usage-based (hit a cap), or sales-assisted (PQL signal triggers outreach). For $20K+ ACV products, sales-assisted is the default because pure self-serve conversion at enterprise pricing is rare.

**Benchmark targets (Poyar, $20K+ ACV B2B SaaS):**
- Signup → activation: 30-50%
- Activation → PQL: 15-30%
- PQL → paid (sales-assisted): 15-25%
- Free trial → paid (unassisted): 2-5%
- Free trial → paid (hybrid): 10-20%

## Pricing page guidance

What works: lead with the use case not the plan name, make the middle tier the default anchor, show outcomes at each tier not just features, limit to 3 tiers, FAQ section that handles the top 5 objections before they become sales calls, and show an enterprise "starting from" floor instead of just "Contact us."

What doesn't: 40-row feature comparison tables, "Contact us" with zero price signal, plans with no clear differentiation story, or burying the free trial option.

## Price increase communication

Any price increase needs internal briefing (sales and CS) and external notice (30-60 days minimum). Poyar's rule: never raise prices and reduce value in the same announcement.
