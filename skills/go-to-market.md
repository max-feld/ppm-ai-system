# Go-to-Market & Launch Planning

A launch planning skill built on four operator frameworks applied in order: diagnose, tier, plan, narrate.

## The problem it solves

Product launches in B2B SaaS have a consistency problem. Every launch feels like the first one. The PMM reinvents the messaging matrix, channel plan, enablement sequence, and alignment process from scratch every time. And most launches are oversized for ego reasons, undersized for pipeline reasons, or both.

## The workflow

**Step 1: Diagnose (Ritson).** No launch plan starts until four questions are on the table: What's changed that makes this the right launch right now? What do we actually know about how the audience will respond? What changes if this launch underperforms? What are we deliberately not doing? A launch without diagnosis is a press release with a calendar invite.

**Step 2: Tier the launch (Oakley).** Jason Oakley's tiering system sizes the effort before work begins:

- **Tier 1 (major launch):** Full marketing push, press, events, enablement overhaul, often a category claim. 8-12+ weeks.
- **Tier 2 (feature launch):** Blog + email + social + sales brief. 2-4 weeks. Meaningful but not category-shifting.
- **Tier 3 (minor update):** Changelog entry, in-app notification, CS heads-up. 2-5 days.

The rule: don't oversize. Most "T1" launches are actually T2 with executive ego attached. When everything is announced as big, nothing is.

**Step 3: Plan (Kramer).** Emily Kramer's framework turns audiences into accountable plans. For each launch: name 1-3 audiences explicitly, pick a primary (trying to serve all audiences with one launch is the #1 failure mode), set measurable goals per audience, define strategies, then map tactics. Every tactic ladders back to a goal, every goal to a named audience.

**Step 4: Narrate (Raskin).** For T1 launches, the skill builds the strategic narrative before the launch brief. The Raskin arc: name the undeniable shift → show the stakes → tease the promised land → position the product as "magic gifts" → show evidence. The product enters at step four, not step one. This becomes the spine of the keynote, the blog hero, and the sales narrative.

## The launch brief

The skill produces a structured launch brief: diagnosis (Ritson), what we're launching, audiences and goals (Kramer), strategic narrative (Raskin, T1 only), core message with proof points, competitive positioning implications, success metrics with measurement window, launch assets checklist scaled by tier, team owners, risk register, and timeline working backwards from launch date.

## Sales enablement

A launch only works if sales can use it in a live deal. The skill builds enablement alongside the launch brief: the new narrative structured as a Dunford sales pitch arc (insight → old way → new way → why we're best → proof), objection handling updates, battlecard updates for affected competitors, internal FAQ pre-answering the top rep questions, what not to say, and a training plan with dates and formats.

## The retrospective

Every T1 and T2 launch gets a written retrospective within 4 weeks: metrics vs. target, what worked, what didn't, what surprised us, what we'd do differently. The retrospective updates the context file's diagnosis section so the next planning cycle starts smarter.

## What this produced

A major product re-launch using this system hit 30% month-one adoption and 18% inbound demand lift. The launch framework became the template the entire marketing org now runs against. Enablement materials were ready before the sales team knew they needed them, which meant reps were selling in week one instead of week three.
