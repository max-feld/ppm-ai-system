# Buyer Research & VoC Synthesis

A research skill that starts with the decision the research will inform, not the data-gathering plan.

## The problem it solves

PMMs sit on a mountain of qualitative data they barely use. Sales call recordings, win/loss interviews, onboarding surveys, support conversations, NPS verbatims, review site data. Individually, each is an anecdote. Collectively, they're a positioning goldmine. The bottleneck is synthesis: turning 50 hours of call recordings and 200 survey responses into the five insights that actually change your GTM.

The old process took 3-4 weeks. This skill compresses it to 5-7 days while producing deeper synthesis (more sources processed) and more honest results (AI doesn't have confirmation bias, though it has other biases you need to watch for).

## The diagnosis step (Ritson)

Before designing any research, the skill forces the diagnosis: "What decision is this research going to inform, and what would have to be true for that decision to go a particular way?"

If you can't answer that, the research is premature. The most common failure mode in PMM research is gathering customer data with no decision in mind, then producing a deck nobody acts on.

Once the decision is named, the skill routes to the right workflow:

| Decision | Deliverable |
|----------|------------|
| Who do we sell to / which segment to prioritize? | ICP definition or refinement |
| What does our buyer actually care about? | Buyer personas + JTBD map |
| Why are we winning or losing? | Win/loss analysis (Dunford-structured) |
| How do buyers actually make this decision? | Buying journey map |
| Is this messaging landing? | Message testing (Laja/Wynter methodology) |
| What are we hearing across these calls/transcripts? | VoC synthesis |

## The four workflows

**Win/loss analysis (Dunford-structured).** Frames interviews around Dunford's positioning components rather than generic "why did you buy" questions (which surface post-rationalized answers). Then layers on Moesta's forces of progress: push (what was wrong with the old way), pull (what attracted them to the new way), anxiety (what worried them about switching), and habit (what kept them stuck). For won deals, which forces overcame inertia? For lost deals, which forces won? This surfaces what you have to overcome, not just what you have to offer.

**JTBD + Persona mapping (Moesta).** Digs into the purchase moment using the Moesta interview structure: "Take me back to when you first realized you had this problem." The output is a three-dimensional job map (functional jobs, emotional jobs, social jobs) plus validated personas grounded in at least 5 interviews each, not assumptions.

**VoC synthesis.** Processes raw transcripts through a standard frame (who is this person, situation before, trigger, alternatives evaluated, why they chose, outcome, what they wish was different). Then synthesizes across sources with a 3-source minimum for themes (anything appearing fewer than 3 times is an anecdote, not a pattern). Separately mines for the exact language buyers use, because the gap between how you talk about your product and how buyers describe their problem is where bad messaging lives.

**Message testing (Laja/Wynter).** Uses Laja's diagnostic questions: "In your own words, what does this do?" (clarity), "Who is this for?" (audience fit), "Why would you choose it over alternatives?" (differentiation), "What's confusing?" (clarity diagnosis). Scales the method to the stakes: Wynter panel for high-stakes messaging, 5-second tests for medium, A/B tests in market for low, informal 1:1s for exploratory.

## Quality standards

Strong research leads with the insight, not the methodology. Uses verbatim quotes (don't paraphrase customer language, it loses precision). Includes disconfirming evidence (things that challenge the hypothesis). Connects every finding back to the decision the research was meant to inform. Is clear about sample size and who was interviewed.

Weak research is personas based on assumptions rather than data, insights too generic to act on ("buyers care about ROI"), or testing messaging only with internal stakeholders instead of ICP-matched buyers.
