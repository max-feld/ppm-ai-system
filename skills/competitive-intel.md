# Competitive Intelligence

A CI skill that goes deeper than most competitive programs by applying two lenses most CI work misses: Dunford's broader alternatives set and Sharp's distinctive brand assets.

## The problem it solves

Most competitive intelligence is shallow. A PMM Googles a competitor, reads their marketing page, builds a feature comparison table, and calls it a battlecard. This misses three things:

1. The buyer's real consideration set includes do-nothing, in-house teams, and agencies, not just named competitors. A battlecard built only against a named competitor misses the actual deal dynamics.
2. Marketing pages routinely overstate capabilities. Product documentation is the source of truth, and the gap between marketing claims and shipped product is some of the most actionable intel you can put in a battlecard.
3. Competitive advantage in mature markets is as much about what cues buyer recall (brand assets) as it is about feature differentiation.

## The research layers

The skill researches competitors across four layers, not just the marketing page:

**Layer 1: Marketing sources (what they claim).** Website, product pages, pricing, blog, changelog, G2/Capterra reviews, job postings, LinkedIn. Standard CI work stops here.

**Layer 2: Product documentation (what they've actually built).** Help centers, API docs, release notes with dates, status pages. This is where the real intel lives. The skill looks for feature status labels ("beta," "coming soon," "early access"), marketing-to-docs gaps (what's on the marketing page but missing from the docs), default states (opt-in vs. opt-out), credit limits and usage caps that marketing pages omit, and timelines that have slipped. The goal isn't to trash-talk the competitor. It's to arm sales with the right questions: "Ask them to demo [feature X] live" is more effective than "they're lying about [feature X]."

**Layer 3: Distinctive brand assets (Sharp).** The visual, verbal, and narrative cues that trigger category recall. For each competitor: their logo and visual identity, their tagline and signature phrases, their recurring narrative, whether the founder is part of the brand identity, and any events or campaigns they're known for. Then the question that matters: what assets do we own that they don't, and vice versa? A competitor with strong distinctive assets is more mentally available than their feature set would suggest.

**Layer 4: Review data.** G2 and review site analysis, looking for most common praise (genuine strengths to acknowledge), most common complaints (real weaknesses to surface), and who's leaving reviews (their actual customer base vs. your ICP).

## The battlecard structure

The output is a Dunford-structured battlecard designed for a rep under pressure in a live deal:

- **One-line summary** of who the competitor is and who they win with
- **Where in the alternatives set they sit** (Dunford), including adjacent alternatives and status-quo options also in play for the same deal
- **When you'll see them** (deal types, buyer profiles, stages)
- **Their distinctive assets** (Sharp: visual, verbal, narrative)
- **Their strengths** (honest, because sales won't trust a card that minimizes these)
- **Their weaknesses** (structural limits, with guidance on how to surface them naturally)
- **How we win** (mapped to unique attributes, not generic claims)
- **Talk track** (conversational, not scripted)
- **Common objections and responses** (using real buyer language, not corporate-speak)
- **Landmines to avoid** (what not to say or do in a competitive deal)
- **Proof points** that directly counter the competitor's main claims
- **Marketing-to-docs gaps** (specific claims unsupported by product documentation, with evidence)

## Maintenance cadence

The skill runs ongoing monitoring for priority competitors: weekly checks on blog, changelog, pricing, and review sites. Quarterly deep refreshes per competitor. And a quarterly Sharp asset audit: revisiting each competitor's distinctive assets to catch identity refreshes, new CEPs, or new language.

## Results

12 battlecards maintained with this system. 75% reduction in time spent on ad-hoc competitive research requests. Sales went from "I don't know what to say about Competitor X" to self-serve competitive answers within two quarters.
